Drawing Outside the Lines

Creativity as rebellion. Making things that don't fit neatly into categories. The intersection of art, technology, and whatever feels right.

"You don't find new ideas in the center. You find them where things collide."

When we sat down to talk about writing as both communication and art, Kahran said something that stuck: "There's photography as documentation and there's photography as art and then there's photography as narrative." The same split applies to writing, to code, to design -- any medium where someone can be working in service of a message or working in service of expression, and sometimes doing both at once. The interesting work, as he put it, lives in the gap between what you want to say and what you actually say. Divya pushed on this, asking how writing is different from communication, and what unfolded was a conversation about playing with form versus playing with meaning, and how the most interesting creators are usually doing both simultaneously.

Computational Mama gave us a beautiful example of what that looks like in practice. She started a secret Instagram account to share her coding experiments because she did not want to mix it with her main work as a designer and culture sector professional. But then the moniker took on a life of its own. "I slogged my butt off for so many years in the museum and culture sector management kind of roles," she said. "And I have really no recognition for it. But I just felt I should embrace it as much as I want to." She described a kind of superhero identity effect -- not becoming a different person, but giving herself "a bit more space with this moniker, to be myself, to sort of say what I want to." Her path from design to development to creative coding dissolved the boundary between technical and creative work entirely.

"The tools that we design design us."

Katieann Vogel, who has been everything from an outdoor adventure guide to a professional limerick writer to a poet, told us that what drew her to our podcast was not the vocabulary or the topics but the tone. "I just heard a lot of passion in how you two were speaking to each other," she said. "The mood and the tone is extremely inviting and a little bit different than anything else I'm listening to." That observation landed for us because it gets at something we believe deeply: the line between creative and technical, between art and strategy, is mostly a line people draw for institutional convenience. The real energy happens where those things collide.

In our conversation about how LLMs have changed our thinking, Divya offered a framing that keeps coming back to us: "The tools that we design design us." The way we use language affects how we think, the way we design buildings affects how we feel, and now the way we use AI tools is reshaping how we approach problems. Kahran talked about using ChatGPT to recall a word he could not remember -- in abeyance -- and how that tiny moment felt like a shift in his relationship with his own memory. But the deeper point was not about AI replacing anything. It was about noticing that these tools are changing the shape of our thinking in ways we are only beginning to understand, and that paying attention to that change is itself a creative act.

Here is where we have explored this further.

"The tools that we design design us."
