Play as Practice

The projects that haunt you are the ones worth doing

Doing things you are not good at. Bonding through the act of making something together. Why the side projects nobody asked for end up teaching you the most.

Kahran had never built a comic before. Divya had never had a co-creator in that medium. As Kahran put it on the podcast, "after months and years of working together, we decided to explore a more creative pursuit." Neither of them expected what happened next. The making itself — figuring out panels, arguing about what a character would say, cutting dialogue down to four words — became a different kind of conversation than any client project had ever produced. "Writing for the comics was really fun," Kahran said. "I feel like that wasn't really that different than writing short dialogue." But what surprised him was how much he was still learning: "I feel like I'm still at a point of rapid progression, and it's very fun." That feeling — of being bad at something and improving fast — is the engine of play as practice.

What made the comics work was not skill. It was that both of them were willing to be beginners. Divya noticed it right away: Kahran's brain worked differently in a visual medium. "I would cut with the axe and you can cut with a scalpel," he told her. "You can remove a word or two rather than — I would try and change the whole thing." She had craft in the medium; he had a completely fresh perspective. The collision produced something neither could have made alone. As Kahran reflected, "bonding over the creation process is really a bonding moment. It's so arduous and fun, but it can be painful at times."

The play — the actual making of the thing — was not separate from the serious work they do for clients. It was the same muscle. The comic-making taught them things about pacing, about visual communication, about when to leave things unsaid, that fed directly back into how they think about pitch decks and brand narratives. Every website hero section, every pitch deck slide, every brand tagline is essentially a comic panel: a constrained space where words and visuals need to work together.

"The projects that haunt you are the ones worth doing."

Divya named the feeling that sits at the center of all creative side projects: "Creative work always has that thread in it. There is this sort of playful self-indulgence that's always there. It's like, I'm getting away with something." That sense of getting away with something is not frivolous. It is the signal that you are working on something that matters to you, not just something that was assigned to you. When a friend reacted to Divya's work pace with surprise — "what, this is what work feels like?" — Divya's answer was instant: "Yeah, this is what work feels like if you're actually enjoying what you're doing."

Then there is the question of why people hold back from making things. In the episode on conforming to be unique, Kahran talked about visiting the Bahamas and noticing "this desire to fit in with your community, which felt to me like overshadowing almost this desire to stand out." Divya connected it to something deeper: the reason people stay in closets of all kinds is not just fear of harsh rejection. "There's also the social rejection of the soft variety where it's like, but I'm not like these people. And they have been my people for the last 25 years." Making something creative — a comic, a zine, an installation — is an act of standing out. Play gives you permission to do it without the full weight of identity on your shoulders.

The biggest lesson was simpler than any of this. Making something together that is not for a client changes the partnership. It gives you a shared creative vocabulary that you did not have before. It builds trust of a different kind — the kind that comes from being vulnerable about what you think is good and what you think is not working. And that trust, built in play, is what makes the serious work better.
