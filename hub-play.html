Play as Practice

The projects that haunt you are the ones worth doing

Joy and rigor aren't opposites. The best work happens when you actually care — and when you play. Comics, zines, installations, and the creative work we make because we can't not make it.

When we started making comics together, something shifted.

Kahran had never built a comic before, and for Divya, having a co-creator in that medium was entirely new. As Kahran described it, "bonding over the creation process is really a bonding moment. It's so arduous and fun, but it can be painful at times."

Podcast Creating comics together with Kahran and Divya →

What we found was that the play -- the actual making of the thing, figuring out the panels, arguing about what a character would do -- that was not separate from the serious work we do for clients. It was the same muscle. The comic-making taught us things about pacing, about visual communication, about when to leave things unsaid, that fed directly back into how we think about pitch decks and brand narratives.

Comic The Friend →

"The projects that haunt you are the ones worth doing."

Remy Siu, the creative director of 1000xResist, gave us one of the best descriptions of what play-as-practice looks like at scale. His pitch: "What if a kind of immigrant teenage girl became the most important person on earth and spawned a whole society based around her personality and her baggage?"

That is not a corporate brief. That is someone playing with an idea until it became something real. Their team was tiny, everyone wore multiple hats, and Remy came from theater and music -- not traditional game development. The game became one of the most celebrated of its year. Sometimes the most rigorous work comes from people who refused to stay in their lane.

Podcast Creating a Successful Game in 1000xResist with Remy Siu →

In our conversation about game design, Kahran noted how rare it is to just talk about the process of making a thing with another creator. "You and I don't talk to each other as creators all the time," he told Divya. She agreed, but added that what they do is "talk to each other at multiple levels" -- swinging between the strategic and the personal, the analytical and the intuitive, sometimes in the same sentence. That multi-level conversation is what play enables. It loosens up the thinking so you can move between registers without getting stuck in any one of them.

Then there is the paradox of trying to be unique. In our episode on conforming to be unique, Kahran talked about visiting the Bahamas and noticing "this desire to fit in with your community, which felt to me like overshadowing almost this desire to stand out." Divya connected it to something deeper: the reason people stay in closets of all kinds is not just fear of harsh rejection. "There's also the social rejection of the soft variety where it's like, but I'm not like these people. And they have been my people for the last 25 years."
