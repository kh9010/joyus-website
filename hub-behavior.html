How People Actually Behave

The gap between how people think they act and how they actually act. Behavioral design, psychology, and the messy reality of human decision-making.

Early in the podcast, we did an episode on cognitive versus emotional experiences, and Kahran started pulling a thread that kept unraveling.

"I realized that I don't have a solid model of failure," he said. "Like, what does good failure look like? I don't know."

Podcast Thinking on Cognitive vs Emotional Experiences & Models for Failure →

"We build models for success all the time. But we almost never build models for what good failure looks like."

Even though that is where most of the actual learning happens. The question he kept circling was: what separates making a foolish decision from being a fool? "Just making a creative thing does not make you a creative person. But there is something that makes you a creative person. So then what is the mindset that a fool has?"

This led us into thinking about how pain and learning are actually wired together. In our episode on pain and emotional signaling, Divya brought up the concept of eustress and distress. "The parts of our brain that are sensitive to pain are also the parts which are close to making new memories," she explained. "Quite literally, we learn through pain." She talked about watching a young child learn to walk -- if he falls down and nobody reacts with alarm, he does not know he was supposed to be upset. We have formed these understandings of: I have this sensation, this sensation means this, I should then react in this way. So much of what we call behavior is actually learned interpretation layered on top of a physical signal.

Podcast Thinking on Pain & Emotional Signaling →

When we talked about designing expectations, we got into how this plays out in product design. Kahran referenced Seth Godin's definition of quality as "whatever meets the spec" and how useful that framing is as a guard against perfectionism. But the deeper insight was about the gap between what someone expected and what they experienced. "Sometimes we get stuck because we're not understanding what expectation we are trying to meet," Divya observed. "And whose, and is that even a relevant expectation?"

Podcast Thinking on Designing Expectations →

In our conversation about decision-making under uncertainty, we wrestled with how people actually make choices when they lack complete information. Divya talked about how even deciding on a brand mascot -- a potato, in their case -- required thinking not about whether it was the right answer, but about what emotion drove the decision. "What does it represent? What made you choose a quirky decision right now?" The meta-analysis of the decision matters as much as the decision itself.

And in our loyalty episode, Kahran noticed that Nike had created multiple ways for him to "hang out with the brand" -- the shoes, the Run Club app, the shopping experience -- each touchpoint deepening the relationship without any single one feeling like a hard sell. That is how real loyalty works: not through one grand gesture, but through many small, well-designed encounters.

"The same work can succeed or fail depending entirely on the expectations surrounding it."
