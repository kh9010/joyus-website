To show how the product feels to someone arriving for the first time, we've walked through it with Bhavna — a fictional user, based on the patterns Tatsam's team sees in real onboarding data. No real user's story is reproduced here. The screens on the left are what Bhavna sees. The notes on the right are the design decisions behind each one — written small, because the product's job is to disappear.

— Joyus Studio

Moment 1 —

Opening the app

A pine branch. A mountain. Two buttons. I don't know why I was bracing.

— Bhavna
design note

No checklist, no symptom intake, no medical imagery on first paint.

Two photographs — a plant cradled in a hand, a horizon at dusk — and two verbs: LET'S START, LOG IN. No mood slider, no PHQ-9, no "how bad is it today?" Category convention pushes hard toward symptom-first intake; we threw it out. Someone opening this app for the first time is already carrying the feeling that something is wrong — a screen that confirms it before saying hello is the fastest way to close the app. The first paint shows evidence of care without asking for anything in return. Tradeoff: a new user doesn't immediately know what the app does. We decided ambiguity costs less than a medicalised welcome.

Moment 2 —

The first question: what to call you

It said nickname. I wasn't ready for my real name. I typed the one my sister uses.

— Bhavna
design note

The first input is identity, and it's optional.

One line of text — no last name, no email, no "please verify." We tested flows that front-loaded account creation; the drop-off between "What should we call you?" and "Enter your email" was the cliff everything fell off. Account data moves later, after the user has had a low-stakes exchange with the product first. The placeholder — "you can use your name or a nickname" — gives permission to lie. For someone whose relationship to their own name is loaded (a diagnosis attached, a family that uses it wrong), the nickname option isn't a cute touch, it's the reason they continue. Tradeoff: no verified identity on screen one. What we keep: the person.

Moment 3 —

Five faces, not ten numbers

Five faces. Not a slider. I picked the one in the middle. I didn't have to decide if it was a three or a four.

— Bhavna
design note

Feeling isn't a score.

The category default is a 0–10 slider — clean number, charts well, tracks against PHQ-9 / GAD-7. After a week with Tatsam's clinical advisors we walked away from it for the first check-in. A slider asks you to introspect and quantify in the same motion; five faces let you gesture at a state without committing to a rank you might not trust. The faces were redrawn three times — we pulled back expressiveness each round, because anything too cartoon-legible started to feel like it was telling you how to feel. The tradeoff: five buckets is a coarser signal than ten. We recovered that resolution in Moment 5, where rating has earned its place. Here, precision would be false precision.

Moment 4 —

The follow-up, when the face is sad

It didn't tell me I was in crisis. It didn't offer me a hotline. It asked if I wanted to write about it. I didn't, and that was also fine.

— Bhavna
design note

When mood is low, the product offers — it doesn't insist.

Tap a lower face: no crisis banner, no hotline, no pivot to a breathing exercise. Just a text field, a microphone, and a DONE button that exits cleanly. Most category leaders escalate here — a defensible liability posture, but one that trains users to under-report to avoid the interruption. The copy ("Would you like to write about your feelings right now?") is an offer with a real no: DONE isn't "skip," it's a full exit. The microphone exists because typing while low is hard. Genuine risk language does route through a separate safety layer, but that's a clinical pathway, not an onboarding screen. The tradeoff: some users who need more won't be pushed toward it. The product's position is that pushing here loses more people than it helps.

Moment 5 —

Ranking life, not rating it — and then, carefully, rating it

First they asked me to put them in order. Family first. Work further down than I expected. Then, only after I'd ordered them, they asked how satisfied I was in each — one to five. By then I'd already decided which ones I cared about. The scores were for me, not for a diagnosis.

— Bhavna
design note

Ranking before rating.

Two screens, deliberately in this order. First: drag-to-reorder eight life areas — no numbers, just "put these in the order that matters to you right now." Only once the ranking is committed does the second screen appear with a 1-to-5 slider per area. Most life-assessment instruments rate first and infer priority from the ratings. We inverted it because rating an area you haven't decided you care about is how people end up with a wheel-of-life score that feels like a diagnosis. Ranking is relational ("family before career, right now") and costs less than scoring ("my family life is a 2"). By the time sliders appear, the user has already told themselves what the list means — numbers land as self-knowledge, not self-audit. The 1–5 scale (not 1–10) keeps the cognitive weight of this moment below the threshold where new users close the app.

Moment 6 —

The first practice, finished

Awesome. Hurray. It was a little too cheerful. But I noticed I wasn't bracing anymore.

— Bhavna
design note

The ending is light, almost childlike — the only moment the voice lifts.

The whole onboarding is low-volume — no exclamation marks, no "great job," no emoji. On this screen alone the voice lifts: "Awesome! You just finished your first practice!" and a button that says HURRAY! We argued about it. The restrained register is what makes Tatsam feel adult; breaking it risks off-brand brightness. We kept the lift because the alternative — "Practice complete." — treats finishing a first gratitude journal as a transaction, and it isn't. For someone who spent six screens quietly wondering if they're the kind of person who can do this, the tonal shift is the signal that they did. The illustration echoes the opening photograph and closes the loop visually. Cost: the jump in register is noticeable — which is exactly what Bhavna notices. That was the point.
