Game Design Thinking

Every product is a game someone chooses to keep playing

Game design principles — levels, feedback loops, progression, onboarding — are not just for games. They change how we think about building products, creating experiences, and making serious work feel lighter.

Remy Siu, the creative director of 1000xResist, built one of the most celebrated games of its year with a tiny team where everyone wore multiple hats. His pitch was not a product brief: "What if a kind of immigrant teenage girl became the most important person on earth and spawned a whole society based around her personality and her baggage?" That is someone playing with an idea until it became something real. Remy came from theater and music, not traditional game development. The game won multiple awards. Sometimes the most rigorous work comes from people who refused to stay in their lane.

What struck Kahran and Divya about Remy's story was not the awards. It was the working conditions. Remy described the indie game creator's day: grant writing, meetings, freelancing to pay the bills, and then realizing the creative work has not happened yet. "When do you do the art? The day is over. Then you're like, oh, oh shit. And then you start to stress out, then you get a little bit existential crisis." Product people will recognize this feeling. The operational work consumes the day. The actual building, the actual designing, gets pushed to the margins. The game designers who build something people remember are the ones who protect the space where the real making happens.

Divya Tak

When Kahran and Divya discussed game design together, the conversation turned to the ethics of attention. Kahran raised how Duolingo "goes into the negative realm sometimes. They will use guilt. They'll try to trigger some of these other kinds of emotions that are not always just positive emotions." Divya noted how the justification is always the same: "The ends always justify the means," she said skeptically. There is a fundamental difference between a game you choose to pick up and put down and a product that follows you around all day demanding your attention. A well-designed game respects the boundary between the player's world and the game world. It creates an experience so compelling that people come back because they want to, not because they were guilted or nudged into returning.

Divya asked whether the industry is waking up to the idea that attention is not sustainable. "I don't think so," she answered herself. "People who used to use their devices a lot are a lot more mindful and actively trying to cut down the time. Although I do not see more responsible behavior from the makers of these things." Kahran was blunt: "There are zero incentives to care about the ethics of something. You gain nothing out of being ethical and you lose everything in the short term." This is where game design offers product people something valuable. In product terms, the game designer's lesson is the difference between retention built on value and retention built on manipulation.

The conversation also surfaced how games teach resilience in ways that schools and workplaces rarely do. Divya noted that kids who grow up playing games develop higher failure resilience — not because games reward them for failing, but because the cost of trying again is nearly zero. "Games that teach people that failure is good are brutal about the failure," she said. "It's just that it's a zero cost to come back and try." The best product onboarding works the same way: it makes the cost of experimentation low enough that people explore rather than freeze.

The game designer's toolkit for product people comes down to a few principles drawn from these conversations. First, constrain the scope aggressively — a small team with multiple hats can build something extraordinary if the scope is clear. Second, respect the player's time — build something worth coming back to, not something that punishes people for leaving. Third, let the narrative do the work — the story people tell each other about your product is more powerful than any feature list. And finally, the hardest one: make room for the art. If the operational work fills every day and the creative work never happens, the product will be competent but lifeless.
