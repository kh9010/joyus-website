Doing Serious Things Unseriously: A Manifesto

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

On the creative process episode, Divya described a conversation with a friend about a collaborative project. They had been working for over an hour when Divya checked the time and mentioned it. Her friend's reaction stuck with her: "She was just like, what, this is what work feels like? This is totally not what work is supposed to feel like." Divya's response was immediate: "Yeah, this is what work feels like if you're actually enjoying what you're doing. This is like, what, I'm getting away with something." And then she named the thing: "I feel like creative work always has that thread in it. There is this sort of playful self-indulgence that's always there. It's like, I'm doing this because I find it fun."

That feeling of getting away with something is the beating heart of serious creative work done unseriously. It is the comic you make on a Saturday that teaches you more about visual storytelling than a month of client briefs. It is the podcast episode that meanders into territory you did not plan for and lands somewhere better. It is the game you build because the medium fascinates you, not because a client asked for it.

"Creative work always has that thread in it. There is this sort of playful self-indulgence that's always there. It's like, I'm getting away with something."

When Kahran and Divya talked about making comics together, the conversation kept circling around this question of what separates a creator from an entrepreneur. Divya put it simply: "On a value level, at least to me, it feels like an entrepreneur is thinking about growing the business and a creator is thinking about making amazing things." She compared it to Miyazaki declaring Boy and the Heron would be his last film, then appearing at the Ghibli offices with a new script. "The man might not even be alive to make the film. There is just a different kind of feeling when you see, oh, I have made this. And then you just sort of put your thumbprint on it all."

The creative process episode also surfaced the tension between novelty and mastery. Divya asked Kahran how he thinks about the tug of war between seeking novelty and getting to perfection. "When you're someone who's good at a lot of things and a lot of things can come easily to you, it's hard to balance what's coming from not liking to do things you're not good at versus staying in your strengths." This is the quiet battle that every creative person fights. Play pulls you toward the new. Craft pulls you toward the deep. The manifesto here is that you need both, and that the play is what keeps the craft from becoming mechanical.

Kahran described how reading his own poetry in public gave him a new set of challenges after years of writing in private. "There's a way you're conveying more meaning with it when you're reading it. It doesn't give you the same sort of satisfaction that it once did." Divya named it plainly: "Same activity is less satisfying. What else is the definition of boredom?" But boredom is not failure. Boredom is the signal that the play needs to move. The same form that once felt electric has become routine, and the only cure is to find the next form that makes you feel like you are getting away with something again.

"People often say don't change the format of what you are making. But if you keep doing the same thing forever, it will stagnate at some point. The same cast doesn't keep doing the same Broadway show forever."

This manifesto is short because manifestos should be. Do serious things unseriously. Make the comic. Build the game. Record the podcast episode that has no obvious business purpose. Approach your work with the curiosity of someone who is exploring a medium for the first time. As Divya said about her own practice: "I wonder how thread behaves with this thing in this situation is the thought in my mind." Not what will this sell for. Not what will the client think. Just: I wonder how this behaves. That wonder is the practice. Everything else is just the output.
