The Accidental Brand: How Joyus Studio's Visual Identity Emerged from Side Projects

Joyus Studio's visual identity was never planned. It emerged from comics, side projects, and conforming to your own weirdness.

The branding of Joyus Studio was never a branding exercise. On the podcast about making comics together, Kahran described the collaborative creative process as something fundamentally new for both of them: "For me, building comics for the first time has been incredible and so much learning. And for Divya, having a co-creator to build a comic with was a new experience and something that she also found very rewarding." They did not set out to define a visual identity. They set out to make something together that was not for a client. The identity emerged from that making.

Divya reflected on the decision-making episode about how the Joyus branding had been deliberately kept flexible: "We are going to probably try and be much more flexible with our branding. We don't want to do just one logo and then it's done for the next five years." This was not a strategy document. It was a practical acknowledgment that they did not know who they were yet, and that pretending they did would lock them into something premature.

"There is just a different kind of feeling when you see, oh, I have made this. And then you just sort of put your thumbprint on it all."

The conformity episode surfaced something deeper. Kahran described visiting the Bahamas and watching how wealthy New England families navigated the tension between fitting in and standing out: "There was this desire to fit in with your community, which felt like overshadowing almost what otherwise would be a desire to stand out." Divya contrasted this with queer culture: "In queer circles, people are always trying to be the brightest peacock." For Joyus, a studio run by a queer man from Seattle and a woman from Rajasthan, the visual identity was never going to come from fitting in with what consulting studios are supposed to look like.

Kahran described loss aversion as a force in branding: "You have this thing that you know, you have a community that you know. Am I going to give this up for an uncertain future?" When you build a brand from scratch as a small studio, the temptation is to borrow the visual language of bigger, more established studios. To signal that you belong in the same category. But borrowing that language means giving up the thing that makes you different before you even know what that thing is.

The comics became the vehicle for discovering it. Divya's visual style, her recurring young heroines exploring worlds that push back against them, gave Joyus a visual vocabulary that no amount of branding workshops would have produced. Kahran's writing, trained in poetry and sharpened by the podcast, gave the studio a voice that was intimate and analytical at the same time. Neither of these things was designed. They were made, in the process of making other things.

"Our culture defines the floor and the ceiling of what is norm. We didn't want to just do one logo and be done for the next five years."

Divya's observation about cultural floors and ceilings applies directly to brand identity: "Sometimes we call something non-conformist when it has a very high ceiling, but you can also get a very high floor as well." The floor for what a studio brand is supposed to look like, the minimalist logo, the clean sans-serif, the muted color palette, is very high. Breaking through that floor is not about rebellion. It is about paying attention to what you are actually making and letting that become the identity.

The accidental brand is not an argument against intentional design. It is an argument for making things before you brand them. The identity lives in the work, not in the style guide. Every comic panel, every podcast episode, every side project that seemed like it had nothing to do with the business was quietly building a visual and tonal language that no consultant could have prescribed. The brand was there all along. It just needed the side projects to reveal itself.
