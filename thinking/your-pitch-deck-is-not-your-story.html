Finding Your Story

Your Pitch Deck is Not Your Story (But It Should Contain One)

Most pitch decks present data without a story. Why investor artifacts need narrative structure, not just information.

We have spent a lot of time helping startups strengthen their ideas and take something that might be at the point of being a small idea, a little genesis, and get it into something bigger. Sometimes that means helping think through important artifacts that get created in the process. Sometimes they are pitch decks for investors. Sometimes they are to share the story with potential co-founders or early employees. And sometimes, as with the Smiqql acquisition deck we walked through on the podcast, they are to present a company as appealing for acquisition.

The thing we keep running into is this: founders forget that they know the story. As Kahran puts it, "When you are a founder, when you're really close to the company, you forget that you know the story. You know so many parts already. So you have so much context that you're going to apply when you're thinking about what this thing is trying to tell. You're looking at not just the content actually in it, and you're actually looking beyond that into the content that is not there." That invisible context is the gap between the deck you built and the deck your investor reads.

"You want to have a story that you're presenting, and the information and the data that you're presenting as part of the company are coming as part of that story."

When we looked at the MachPhy investor deck, Divya noticed immediately that "sometimes you want to be super shiny, like you need something to grab the audience, and it's not doing that. Either you can grab them informationally, or you can grab them emotionally, but you need to figure out which one you want to do." The deck felt serious but it did not position the reader. It did not anchor them in anything other than sincerity. And sincerity without story is just data in a slide.

This is a pattern we see again and again. Investor decks are high-stakes artifacts, and because the stakes are high, founders often strip out everything that feels personal or narrative-driven. They lead with logos of companies that have mentioned them, thinking borrowed credibility will carry the weight. But as Kahran observed during the MachPhy review, "First I need to understand what you do before I ask myself, are these people trustable?" The order matters. You have to establish the world first, the state of how things are, the problem you are changing, before you can borrow anyone else's credibility.

With the Smiqql acquisition deck, we found ourselves asking a different set of questions. A key part of the story in an acquisition deck is the customer story. Kahran framed it as two pieces: "One is the business story, which could be the numbers story. What is the revenue? What are the margins? And then there is the customer story of why your customers are likely to stay with you, and why they are likely to recommend you to other people." Most decks we see focus almost entirely on the first piece and barely touch the second.

"Because investor decks are so high stakes in most cases, it's very valuable to quickly communicate more than one thing. Think about what each piece of information is doing here."

When we looked at Cranberry.Fit's website, we saw a related problem playing out in a consumer context. The question kept coming back to whether people could even tell what kind of company this was in the first few seconds. Divya pointed out that "the moment you add personalized anything, chances of it being a B2C brand is much higher," but even with the right signals, the storytelling was not helping people feel seen. Kahran pushed on this: "I just would pose it as a question. Do you feel like this conceptualization helps you feel seen?" The answer revealed a gap between telling someone a solution exists and making them feel like they belong to a community that shares their experience.

The throughline across all of these conversations is the same. Whether it is a pitch deck, an acquisition opportunity, or a brand website, you are not just presenting information. You are telling a story. And the most common failure is not a lack of data or even a lack of authenticity. It is the assumption that the reader already knows what you know. They do not. The deck is not the story. But if you build it right, the story lives inside it, and the reader can find their way in.
