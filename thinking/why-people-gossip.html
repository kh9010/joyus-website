Why People Gossip (And What It Means for Community Design)

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

Kahran opened the kindness vs. niceness episode with a surprising etymology: "Police, polite, policy, political, they all come from the same root word. They come from polis, which means city, like metropolis." He connected this to something called the civilizing process, "this notion that as societies modernized and we started to behave in a more civilized way towards each other, certain things started to come into effect." In the American West during the gold rush, murder and theft surged. What brought those numbers back down, according to this framework, was women and religion. "Those are all the things that lead to society. In polite society, we do police ourselves."

This set up a conversation that went deep into why communities need gossip. Not gossip as cruelty, but gossip as the mechanism by which groups establish norms. Divya made a distinction that clarified everything: "I would say that kindness is more long term. It's probably going to be more beneficial for all parties involved." Niceness, by contrast, is about the immediate moment, about not causing discomfort right now, even at the expense of something more important later.

"There was this other person who was very, very nice, extremely nice, but you could just never tell what he is actually thinking or feeling. You always knew where you stood with the first guy. He wasn't a particularly nice person, but you always knew."

Divya told a story about two friends from college. One was not particularly nice but you always knew where you stood with him. The other was extremely nice "but you could just never tell what he is actually thinking or feeling." In communities, the second type creates more damage than the first, because communities need honest signals to function. When niceness replaces honesty, the community loses its ability to self-correct.

This connects to what they discussed about signaling and in-group dynamics. Kahran reflected on how signals can be mixed "because people can be signaling different things with the same things. Which is kind of an interesting place in the world to have ended up." In community design, this means you need ways for members to signal honestly. Gossip, when it is closer to kindness than niceness, is one of those ways. It is how people say "this is what we value here" without having to codify it in rules.

Divya pushed on whether niceness even has consistent meaning: "Whether it's nice or not depends on how we have perceived the potential set of possible actions available in the situation and therefore whether we're prescribing something that is possible or not possible." Telling a young person they have spinach in their teeth is perfectly nice because they have an easy action available to them. Telling an older person the same thing might not feel as nice because the perceived set of available actions is different. The same information, the same intent, the same truth, but the social reading changes entirely.

"Kindness is more long term. It's probably going to be more beneficial for all parties involved. Breaking up is not the nice thing to do. Sometimes it is the kind thing to do."

For community design, the implications are significant. Most online communities are optimized for niceness. The rules are about not being mean, not causing discomfort, not making people feel unwelcome. But communities that last, that actually build something, need kindness more than niceness. They need members who can say hard things in service of the group's long-term health. They need gossip in the anthropological sense: the informal exchange of information about community norms that lets people know what is valued and what is not.

Kahran brought up signaling again: "Signaling is not completely pointless because it's also a building of common language in our culture." A community's gossip is part of its common language. It is how the group teaches new members what matters here. When you design a community that only permits niceness, you cut off one of the most important channels for transmitting values. The gossip does not go away. It just goes underground, where it becomes less kind and more destructive. Better to design for it than to pretend it does not exist.
