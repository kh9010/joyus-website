Pain is a Signal, Not a Problem: What Behavioral Design Gets Wrong About Friction

Friction in design is not always a problem. Eustress, distress, and why removing all pain removes all learning.

Divya opened the pain episode with something that reframed the entire conversation: "The parts of our brain that are sensitive to pain are also the parts which are close to making new memories. So quite literally we learn through pain." She went on to explain the distinction between eustress and distress. "In the current world we just have one word that we use to denote pain, which is stress. But there is the concept of eustress, which is good stress, and there is distress, which is when you're overloading the system. Eustress is required for the system to progress or move forward."

This is the insight that behavioral design keeps getting wrong. The entire field is oriented around reducing friction, removing obstacles, making everything easier. But eustress, good friction, is required for growth. For learning. For the brain to form new circuits. As Divya put it, "Our brain would love to stay in a comfortable position if it can forever. It's lazy and evolutionarily adapted towards just staying wherever it is. But it's only through some amount of introduced eustress that we can grow."

"The parts of our brain that are sensitive to pain are also the parts which are close to making new memories. Quite literally we learn through pain."

Kahran connected this to watching his nephew learn to walk. "It was really interesting how when he falls down, if he doesn't know that he was supposed to be sad, unless you react in a way that you're like, oh my god, he doesn't know that that's the reaction." The sensation is not inherently labeled as pain. We learn to categorize it that way based on the reactions around us. And then we carry those categorizations into adulthood, where they show up as our relationship with friction in every product we use, every experience we design.

Kahran brought this into his own experience with working out: "The difference between different things, right? Like, are you in pain? Are you sore? And I think it's very easy to kind of group that all together and be like, oh, this is net unpleasant." Product designers do the same thing. They group all friction into the category of unpleasant and try to eliminate it. But the friction of learning something new, of building a habit, of engaging deeply with a tool, that is eustress. Removing it does not help the user. It prevents them from building the competence that would make them loyal.

Divya raised the question of whether psychological pain is different from emotional pain is different from physical pain. Kahran's answer was striking: "I think that one tends to manifest the other a lot of times." He described being attracted to yoga partly because "I felt like I would become more settled" and then connected it to how the brain forms pathways around pain avoidance: "Your brain has learned certain pathways to avoid certain kinds of pain and even when those pain points have gone away, your pathway has been structured in just a way that you don't think, oh, I could be taking a shortcut here."

"Your brain has learned certain pathways to avoid certain kinds of pain and even when those pain points have gone away, your pathway has been structured in a way that you don't think you could be taking a shortcut here."

This connects directly to how we think about designing expectations. Kahran observed that the maker of Mario has said "nobody would praise you if you just release a game on the date that you expected it to be released on. Everybody would be happy to wait for two months for a great game." But for products, expectations are different. "If somebody is making a movie, you would be okay with waiting. On the other hand, if it's a product, the expectations are different." The friction tolerance depends entirely on what you are designing and what the user expects from it.

The takeaway is not that friction is good and we should add more of it. The takeaway is that pain is information. It tells you where growth is happening. When a user struggles with a feature, the question should not automatically be "how do we make this easier?" It should be "is this struggle productive?" Is this eustress that is building toward competence? Or is this distress that is overloading the system? The answer changes everything about what you design next.
