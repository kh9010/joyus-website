The Game Designer's Toolkit for Product People

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

In the conversation with Remy Siu, creative director of 1000xResist, something came up that reframed how we think about product design. Remy described the game as "a narrative adventure game where you do a lot of walking and talking and also exploring spaces both based in reality and then doing very light narrative puzzles." The game won multiple awards and critical acclaim, but what struck us was how small the team was. "Our team's very small, so we all kind of wear multiple hats," Remy said. Small team. Multiple hats. Enormous creative ambition. This is not just a game development story. It is a model for product work.

Remy also described what the working day actually looks like for an indie game creator: grant writing, meetings, freelancing to pay the bills, and then realizing the creative work has not happened yet. "When do you do the art? The day is over. Then you're like, oh, oh shit. And then you start to stress out, then you get a little bit existential crisis." Product people will recognize this feeling. The operational work consumes the day. The actual building, the actual designing, gets pushed to the margins.

"If you stop playing the game, you stop playing the game. The game does not stick around in your pocket the whole day, buzzing you five times a day. Five is an underestimate."

When Kahran and Divya discussed game design together, the conversation turned to ethics. Kahran raised how Duolingo "goes into the negative realm sometimes. They will use guilt. They'll try to trigger some of these other kinds of emotions that are not always just positive emotions." Divya noted how the justification is always the same: we are trying to help you learn. "The ends always justify the means," she said skeptically. There is a fundamental difference between a game you choose to pick up and put down and a product that follows you around all day demanding your attention.

Divya asked whether the industry is waking up to the idea that attention is not sustainable. "I don't think so," she said. "Though on the user side, things are changing. People who used to use their devices a lot are a lot more mindful and actively trying to cut down the time. Although I do not see more responsible behavior from the makers of these things." Kahran agreed: "There are zero incentives to care about the ethics of something. You gain nothing out of being ethical and you lose everything in the short term."

This is where game design offers product people something valuable. A well-designed game respects the boundary between the player's world and the game world. It creates an experience so compelling that people come back because they want to, not because they were guilted or nudged into returning. In product terms, this is the difference between retention built on value and retention built on manipulation.

"People who used to use their devices a lot are a lot more mindful and actively trying to cut down the time. I do not see more responsible behavior from the makers of these things."

The game designer's toolkit for product people comes down to a few principles drawn from these conversations. First, constrain the scope aggressively. A small team with multiple hats can build something extraordinary if the scope is clear. Second, respect the player's time. Build something worth coming back to, not something that punishes people for leaving. Third, let the narrative do the work. Remy described how "what players might expect on our Steam page is very different from what players might expect from their friend." The story people tell each other about your product is more powerful than any feature list.

And finally, the hardest one: make room for the art. If the operational work fills every day and the creative work never happens, the product will be competent but lifeless. The game designers who build something people remember are the ones who protect the space where the real making happens, even when everything else is trying to consume it.
