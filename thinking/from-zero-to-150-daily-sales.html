From Zero to 150 Daily Sales: The Unsexy Middle of a Fashion Startup

The unsexy middle of building a fashion startup: practical trust, emotional trust, and why the gap matters.

Divya introduced an idea on the podcast that reframed how we think about the entire customer journey: the distinction between practical trust and emotional trust. "Practical trust is I trust you to do your thing well," she explained. "In a work context, I trust you to perform the task that is assigned to you. And emotional trust is I trust you to have my back if I fail or if I fall." She continued: "You can think of many organizations where one is lacking or the other is lacking in their attitude towards their employees or towards their customers. At least in my experience, most successful brands, most successful relationships in your life, will have a healthy balance of both."

This framing matters enormously for early-stage consumer brands, especially in fashion, where the product is intimate and the competition is relentless. When a new brand goes from zero sales to trying to hit 150 daily orders, there is a long middle where neither practical trust nor emotional trust has been established. The customer has no evidence that your product is well-made. They have no emotional connection to what you stand for. You are starting from nothing on both axes.

"Stories are what tell us values. The stories of an organization and the stories that people choose to retell, those are what are the values and what tell the values of an organization."

In a separate conversation about trust versus confidence, Kahran and Divya went back and forth on where the line falls. Divya felt that "trust is an emotional thing, but confidence is a cognitive thing." Kahran was not so sure. He described trusting a local bakery: you go there multiple times, the quality is consistent, and at some point you stop questioning whether the croissant will be good. That is trust built through repetition. But for a new fashion brand, there is no repetition yet. You are asking someone to make a first purchase based on something else entirely.

Kahran gave another example: investing in a friend who wanted to open a bar. "Do I trust in any of her hard skills necessarily? Not necessarily, not today. What I trust in is her ability to figure out what may come. But really that to me is more of like believing in someone and having confidence in their ability to perform." This is exactly the kind of trust early customers are extending to a new brand. They are not confident in the product yet. They are choosing to believe in the people behind it.

The Nordstrom story came up on the trust episode as a perfect example of how this works at scale. Kahran noted how "the fact that they have this story of accepting a tire being returned, that sets a tone for the organization that then everyone understands. That is a really interesting thought: stories are what tell us values." For a fashion startup in the unsexy middle, the question is not just what stories are you telling your customers. It is what stories are your customers telling each other about you?

"Practical trust is I trust you to do your thing well. Emotional trust is I trust you to have my back if I fail."

The path from zero to 150 daily sales is not a marketing problem. It is a trust-building problem, and it requires working on both kinds of trust simultaneously. On the practical side, you need the product to perform. The fit has to be right, the materials have to hold up, the delivery has to arrive when expected. On the emotional side, you need people to feel like they belong with your brand, like you see them, like the story you are telling is also their story.

Most early-stage brands focus almost entirely on practical trust because it feels measurable. But the brands that break through the unsexy middle are the ones that figure out the emotional side early. They find the story their customers want to retell. And that story, not the product specs, is what takes you from a handful of sales to a business that sustains itself.
