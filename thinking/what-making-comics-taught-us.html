What Making Comics Taught Us About Client Work

What happens when two studio partners start making comics together, and how the lessons fed directly back into client work.

When Kahran and Divya decided to make comics together, it was not a business decision. As Kahran described on the podcast: "After months and years of working together, we decided to explore a more creative pursuit. For me, building comics for the first time has been incredible and so much learning. And for Divya, having a co-creator to build a comic with was a new experience." This was play. Genuine, unpressured play between two people who normally work together in a client-facing context.

What neither of them expected was how directly the lessons would transfer. Kahran reflected on the difference between collaborative creation and solo work: "I think a lot of ways bonding over the creation process is really a bonding moment, because it's so arduous and it's fun. But it can be painful at times. And I also think it's very rare to have these conversations where you are just talking about the process of making a thing and talking to another person as a creator." In client work, they talk to each other as strategists, designers, consultants. Making comics gave them a different register.

"It's very rare to have these conversations where you are just talking about the process of making a thing and talking to another person as a creator."

Divya explored what it means to be conforming even when you think you are being unique. She described visiting the Bahamas and noticing "this desire to fit in with your community, which felt like overshadowing almost what otherwise would be a desire to stand out." In queer circles, she observed, "people are always trying to be the brightest peacock. But outside of that, there is this strong desire for conformity, almost like we need to be with our people and not be isolated." Making comics, a medium that has its own visual conventions and reader expectations, forced them to confront this tension directly. How do you make something that feels like yours without alienating people who expect certain things from the form?

Divya noted how cultural norms set both the floor and the ceiling of creative behavior: "Sometimes we call something non-conformist when we think that it's breaking off of the ceiling. But depending on the cultural norm around you, you can actually get a very high floor as well." In Rajasthan, where she grew up, the floor for financial pragmatism was high and the ceiling for artistic training was low. In Bengal, the opposite. "Most of my Bengali friends were trained in at least one of the arts and it was just so unusual for a Rajasthani kid to be trained in any of the arts formally."

This observation about cultural floors and ceilings turned out to be incredibly useful in client work. When you are helping a brand find its visual identity or its voice, you are working within cultural norms that have both floors and ceilings. The comic work trained them to see those boundaries more clearly and to push on them more deliberately.

"Our culture defines the floor and the ceiling of what is norm. Non-conformity is when you break off the ceiling. But sometimes your culture can actually raise the floor as well."

Kahran described how writing for comics differed from other forms of writing. On the podcast about writing as communication and art, he noted that "writing for the comics was very fun. I feel like that wasn't really that different than writing short dialogue. But writing for the game has been a different paradigm altogether." The constraint of the comic panel, the need to tell a story in a limited visual space with minimal text, is a discipline that transfers directly to client work. Every website hero section, every pitch deck slide, every brand tagline is essentially a comic panel: a constrained space where words and visuals need to work together.

The biggest lesson was simpler than any of this. Making something together that is not for a client changes the partnership. It gives you a shared creative vocabulary that you did not have before. It builds trust of a different kind than delivering projects on time builds. It is the kind of trust that comes from being vulnerable about what you think is good and what you think is not working. And that trust, built in play, is what makes the serious work better.
