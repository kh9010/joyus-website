Designing for the Decision They Almost Made

Most design focuses on the decision people made. The interesting work is in the decisions they almost made.

On the podcast episode about decision-making under uncertainty, the conversation started with something absurd: whether a potato should be the mascot for Joyus Studio. But the absurdity led somewhere real. Divya asked what Kahran would look for to make that kind of decision. His answer: "What is the sort of standout quality of this decision? Like almost a meta analysis of the decision itself. Potato is a bland thing if you just look at a potato. But what made you choose a bland thing seems like a quirky decision." He was not evaluating the potato. He was evaluating the emotion that led to the potato.

This is how most interesting decisions actually work. The final choice is less revealing than the path that led to it. And in product design, the decisions people almost made, the ones they considered and abandoned, are often more instructive than the ones they completed. Divya pushed on this: "What makes something the right feeling or not?" In most branding work, she said, "it comes from a lot of discussion about what they're doing, why they are doing it, what feelings do they want the audience to have. And like that's a very hard question to answer."

"What was the emotion that drove you to weighting the factors in this way? Why were you looking for a quirky decision right now?"

The conversation turned to how finite and infinite thinking shape these decisions differently. Kahran described conversations with vendors where "there's a degree of indefiniteness to the way those people present the relationship they're expecting to have with you. It's very hard to push people on saying what are you going to deliver in what period." The tight box of a finite game makes decisions measurable but risks measuring the wrong things. "Sometimes it's very easy to measure what's easy to measure. And so you're not being able to get a good, accurate read on whether the execution of this idea was bad or you just didn't build it long enough."

Divya noticed the trap: "It's almost like there is a drawback in how people think about bounding their decision making. Your brain would instantly go into finite game mindset and you want to get the thing done." When we design products, we tend to optimize for the decisions people are making right now. But the decisions they almost made, the cart they abandoned, the feature they hovered over, the page they visited three times before leaving, those are the signals worth designing for.

In the caring and being invested episode, they explored why people care about things at all. Divya observed that "care is closely associated with identity. When people talk about being a parent or being a runner, they always talk in terms of themselves or the relational aspect. A part of their identity is attached to this thing." Kahran pushed back: "I wonder if part of that is because you have an attribute that you're expressing in that way. Like, oh, I feel I'm a disciplined person. Well, because of that, I run every day."

"The barrier is the gap between who they are now and who they would become by making this choice."

This is what happens at the moment of an almost-decision. Someone is on the edge of doing something that would change their self-image, even slightly. Buying from a new brand means admitting the old one was not enough. Trying a new tool means acknowledging your current workflow has gaps. The almost-decision is the moment where identity meets action, and identity usually wins.

Designing for this means understanding that the barrier to the decision is not usually information. It is not that people need more features listed or more social proof stacked up. The barrier is the gap between who they are now and who they would become by making this choice. The best product design closes that gap not by pushing harder but by making the new identity feel like a natural extension of the old one. You are not asking them to become someone different. You are showing them they were already on their way.
