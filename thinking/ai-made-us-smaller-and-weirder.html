Drawing Outside the Lines

AI Did Not Replace Us. It Made the Studio Smaller and Weirder.

AI did not replace us. It changed how we think about creativity, translation, and the tools that design us back.

The episode about how LLMs changed their thinking started differently than most podcast episodes. Divya opened by noting that "almost always when we record our podcast, we pretend that we are starting in middle of a conversation but most of the times we are actually having conversation specifically for the podcast. But this time, for the first time, what we have decided is that we are going to record a podcast because we were talking about something interesting and both of us were like, this should be a podcast." The question that provoked it was simple: after months of using AI tools, how has your worldview changed?

Kahran's first answer was small and tactical. He described trying to remember a French phrase, en avance, and being able to just ask ChatGPT. "You can just ask it. I was like, hey, there's a word I'm thinking of that means it's like they're waiting. And it just was like, oh, it appears you're thinking of the term en avance." But Divya wanted to go deeper. She invoked a principle from design: "The tools that we design design us. The way we use language, it affects how we think. The way we design our buildings affects how we feel. All tools that exist, they alter how we relate to the world."

"The tools that we design design us. The way we use language affects how we think. All tools that exist alter how we relate to the world in some way or the other."

Kahran described using AI to translate Turkish poetry, which is something traditional translation tools fail at. "It's so hard to translate poetry online because you're going to get exact translation from Google Translate, which is not what you want." With an LLM, he could ask why a poet chose one kind of tree over another in a specific century, and get contextual answers about cultural symbolism. "It'll talk to you about the trees in the 16th century in Turkey represented this." Divya was immediately inspired: "That has just inspired me to go and look at some Haiku through the lens of GPT because Haiku's tend to do a lot of that where there is a lot of contextual language hidden within those few syllables."

In the writing episode, Kahran distinguished between different categories of writing: communication, documentation, and emotive work. "There are some sets of writing that is for the purpose of communicating ideas. And then there's writing that's very separate from that. There's writing that is meant to be emotive, whether that means by using the meaning of the words themselves, or even the play of the shapes of the words." AI is excellent at the first category. It can communicate clearly, summarize, and structure. It struggles with the second and third. The emotive, the shaped, the playful, that is still human territory.

What this means for the studio is not replacement. It is specialization. The AI handles the communication layer, the first draft of copy, the translation of ideas across languages, the brainstorming of backstories for characters. As Kahran described it, you can ask for "ten different back stories of why these characters could have the powers they do" and use that as raw material for creative decisions that only a human can make.

"I used AI to translate some poetry from Turkish. You can ask why a poet chose one kind of tree over another in a specific century, and it'll talk about what trees represented in 16th century Turkey."

The result is a studio that is smaller and weirder than it would have been without these tools. Smaller because two people can now do work that used to require four or five. Weirder because the time freed up does not go into doing more of the same work. It goes into making comics, building games, translating poetry, exploring mediums that a consulting studio has no business exploring. The AI did not replace the creative work. It replaced the operational drag that was preventing the creative work from happening.

Divya put it best on the podcast: "I have started noticing some changes in the way I think about things because I've been using AI for some time now." The changes are not about efficiency. They are about possibility. When the cost of trying something drops, you try more things. And some of those things turn out to be the most interesting work you have ever done.
