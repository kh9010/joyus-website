The Difference Between Voice and Tone (And Why Most Brands Confuse Them)

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

We spent a lot of time on the podcast talking about how hard it is to describe what you do. It sounds simple, but it is one of the most difficult things for any company or creative person to get right. As Kahran shared on the podcast, "I had a meeting with a new hire at my ed tech company, and she told me that the thing that would make her more successful in her role would be if we would stay the course longer on each idea. And it really took me aback because I felt like we had kind of been staying the course for quite a while." The problem was not that the company was changing direction. The problem was that different people in the company had different understandings of what the company was doing, and without a shared voice, every conversation felt like a new starting point.

This is what happens when voice and tone get confused. Voice is the consistent thing underneath. It is who you are when you strip away the context of the room. Tone is how you adjust for the room. A brand can have one voice and many tones. But when there is no clear voice, every tone feels disconnected from every other tone, and the people inside the company start to feel like the direction keeps shifting.

"For a lot of people in a company, especially in a smaller company, their perception of that company is really shaped by the people they work with on an everyday basis. And if there's not a shared understanding of this is the thing we're doing and why, sometimes it can feel like things are really shifting."

When we talked about signaling and in-group dynamics on the podcast, we started exploring how people use external markers to communicate who they are and who they belong with. Kahran brought up university alumni associations: "If you were to meet someone who had also went to Kharagpur, you would treat them in a different way. You would look at them as being part of this group that you are also part of." The signals we send are not just about identity. They are about belonging. And the same is true for brands.

Divya connected this to how pattern recognition works: "It makes me think of how in ML algorithms, people use clustering as a feature. Certain properties exist together. And this is the pattern matching part of our brain trying to think about it, like, oh, people from this place are trustworthy. Same thing is for buyers, same thing is for sellers, same thing is for collaborators." When a brand's voice is clear, the right people cluster around it. When it is muddled, the signaling breaks down. People cannot tell if they are in the right place.

What makes this even harder is that signaling is not completely pointless or always performative. As Kahran reflected, "Signaling is in some ways a building of common language in our culture. When somebody wears a t-shirt of a brand that they really like, that's part of saying, hey, I like this thing. Anybody who likes this thing can just now know that I like it and we can bond together even though we don't know each other." The same principle applies to how a brand speaks. Its voice is the constant signal. Its tone is the contextual adjustment.

"As somebody who has done a bunch of branding work, it's so frustrating for people to come and give you examples of Nike, Apple and Coke. Do you also recognize they have poured billions of dollars and decades into that branding?"

The practical insight we arrived at through these conversations is that you have to talk to different kinds of people about what you do. Not to get their approval, but to hear yourself describing it in different contexts. As Divya put it, the moment was "really pleasant to be like, oh, okay, now we understand what we do. And now we can talk to other people about it." That understanding is voice. It is the thing that stays the same regardless of who you are talking to. Everything else, the formality, the humor, the level of detail, is tone.

Most brands skip straight to tone. They hire someone to make them sound a certain way before they have figured out what they actually are. And then they wonder why the voice feels inconsistent. The voice was never there to begin with. You cannot adjust what does not exist. Start with who you are. The tone will follow.
