The Two-Person Studio: What We Stopped Doing to Keep Going

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

Somewhere around the two-year mark of our podcast, and deeper into the life of the studio, Kahran started feeling something shift. Not dissatisfaction exactly, but something more subtle. "I wouldn't say interested is the right word," he said on the podcast. "I think what I was thinking about is how the value that you get from something changes over time." The podcast had been a vehicle for learning to articulate. But that skill had improved. The question became: what now?

This is the tension at the heart of any creative practice that also has to sustain itself. When Divya described her relationship with the podcast, she put it differently: "I have certain affinity with mediums. I like making podcasts. It's a medium that appeals to me. It's a bit more on that side where it's like I don't paint to get something out of the painting because the act of painting itself is valuable and interesting enough to me." The process over the product. But when you are running a studio, you cannot always afford to be medium-first. Or can you?

"I feel like as time has gone on, the value has become a skill that I'm more comfortable saying I'm adept at. So now I'm trying to understand: what is the value as we go into our third year?"

The fair compensation episode surfaced a related question. Kahran told the story of taking a sabbatical from tech right before turning thirty to go to culinary school: "It was one of the biggest expenditures I had ever made. They could have bought a car. And I think it was a demonstration to myself that these kind of things, these hobbies are things that are worth investing in for me." That decision was not about the cooking. It was about proving to himself that the things that look frivolous are worth spending time and effort on. "And then as you start to do that over and over again, you start to just think about, okay, what are these things that make me feel like I'm feeling satisfied?"

At Joyus, this has played out in the decision to keep making things that are not strictly client work. Interactive works of poetry. A game. Comics. As Kahran described it, "All of these things are things that we feel like we're creating value. And it's a really different thing than looking and saying, how are we creating the perfect sales pitch to land the perfect client. And I think there's a lot of chasing of that that happens. And that's not satisfying."

Divya drew a distinction that stuck: "I wonder if that is the difference between a creator and an entrepreneur, because both are jobs that require creative thinking. But on a value level, at least to me, it feels like an entrepreneur is thinking about growing the business and a creator is thinking about making amazing things." She compared it to Miyazaki, who said Boy and the Heron would be his last film and then showed up at the Ghibli offices with a new script. "There is just a different kind of feeling when you see, oh, I have made this. And then you just sort of put your thumbprint on it all."

"As our goalposts start to change later in life, maybe we're working for reasons that are outside of just straight compensation. What feels satisfying? What are the things that are going to help you feel compensated?"

What we stopped doing was chasing the version of the studio that looked like what studios are supposed to look like. We stopped trying to scale into something that would require us to stop making things ourselves. The podcast became a space for play and curiosity rather than a marketing channel. Kahran admitted he does not really experiment with form in his poetry or approach projects with that kind of curiosity. "I don't even like, even in my writing, I don't really experiment where I'm like, oh, I wonder what it would feel like just to change the form in this way just to see." But Divya does. And having both energies in the studio, the finisher and the explorer, is part of what keeps it going.

A two-person studio is not a business model anyone would recommend. But it turns out that staying small enough to keep making things, to keep being surprised by what you create, is its own form of sustainability. The things you stop doing are the things that make the remaining things possible.
