Finding Your Story

What We Learned Making a Four-Part Series on Story vs. Telling

Reflections on making a four-part podcast series about the gap between having a story and knowing how to tell it.

The Story vs. Telling series started from a place of real frustration. In the very first episode, Divya said something that set the tone for everything that followed: "I don't think that you have ever felt like the podcast is successful." And Kahran's response was honest: "It's not successful in the way that a piece of media is generally evaluated." The podcast had listeners. It had people who loved individual episodes. But it was not reaching the people they felt it could reach, and neither of them could figure out why.

What they discovered over the course of four episodes was that the problem was not the story. It was the telling. Divya articulated the barrier she was running into: "There's a small niche group that can like my work, but I don't know how to get it to a bigger group. I don't know how to maintain connection with authenticity. When I try to be authentic, the connection breaks." Her friends reading her blog would say it felt like reading her diary, too personal to comment on. Something about the frame around the work was not right.

"I was removing all the emotional core from the story to make it feel more professional. And then there was nothing left."

By the fourth episode, Kahran walked through how he introduces himself and why it works in certain structures. He described his trajectory from computer science to product management to ed tech CEO to shutting down a company to starting Joyus Studio and pursuing an MFA in poetry. It was detailed, it was personal, and it had hooks. But then he realized something: "This brute force method is at odds with what I'm trying to say. Because what I'm trying to say is that I'm finessing the story to give you enough hooks that you can come in with me, but I'm not trying to give you everything."

That was one of the key insights of the whole series. The telling is not about giving everything. It is about giving enough. Enough for someone to find their way in, to feel like they are being invited rather than lectured to. Divya had been stripping the emotional core out of her introductions. Kahran had been brute-forcing his way through with sheer detail. Both approaches had the same problem: they were not meeting the listener where the listener actually was.

The series also surfaced a question about what the work is trying to do. Kahran asked Divya directly what kind of change she was trying to create with her art, and she resisted the framing: "I don't think I'm particularly trying to change too much with what I'm doing. Maybe that's the problem." But Kahran pushed gently. He pointed to the recurring young heroines in her visual work, always against the world, always exploring it in different ways. "Is that part of a story of what your art is telling?" he asked. The answer was not immediate, but it opened something up.

"If I was just creating for me, I would not be putting things out there. I do think I'm creating for connection."

What we learned across these four episodes is that the gap between story and telling is not a craft problem. It is a courage problem. Divya knew what she cared about. Kahran knew what his experience meant. But translating that into something that invites other people in requires a willingness to be seen in a specific way, to choose the hooks you offer and accept that not everyone will grab them. The story is already there. It has always been there. The hard part is deciding how much of it to show, and trusting that showing it will not break the connection you are trying to build.

We are still learning this. The series did not end with answers so much as with a better set of questions. And that, honestly, is what made it worth making.
