Drawing Outside the Lines

Code as Material: When Engineers and Artists Stop Pretending They're Different

By Kahran Singh & Divya Tak

Computational Mama started as a designer and ended up as something else entirely. As she described on the podcast, the moniker began as "a secret-ish Instagram because I didn't want to put this stuff in my main Instagram." Then something happened: "I slogged my butt off for so many years in the museum and culture sector management kind of roles and design roles. And I have really no recognition for it except for my name might be on one wall somewhere in that museum. But I'm getting recognition with this name and it's not like my personality is changing with the name. I'm still just me. But I felt I should embrace it. And it allows me to make more space for myself, the moniker, which in itself is so big."

This idea of a moniker creating space that your given name cannot is deeply connected to how engineers and artists relate to their work differently, or think they do. Computational Mama described what she called the superhero identity effect: "This person was a YouTuber and they were talking about how they find it really challenging to be social. But they made this personality and identity for themselves. And that person is outgoing and keeps people engaged. But the real version of them is totally not that." She was quick to clarify her own experience was different: "It's not like Beyonce and Sasha Fierce. It's very much still me, but I just give myself a bit more space with this moniker."

"I slogged my butt off for years in culture sector roles and have really no recognition for it. But I'm getting recognition with this name. It allows me to make more space for myself."

The conversation about code and creativity went deeper when Computational Mama talked about how working in museums taught her something that tech companies miss entirely. She pointed out that a museum inherently means "a diverse set of people coming into your museum. Diverse backgrounds. But that's something that gets totally missed when we think about scaling projects on the web." She compared it to the largest platforms: "Look at the biggest scale project right now, maybe Facebook or Instagram. Really thriving on diversity and we just totally forget about that. When you're scaling you have to look at diversity. You can't just think about the next billion as one category of demographic."

Katie Ann Vogel, the poet and former outdoor adventure guide, brought a different angle to the same theme. She described what drew her to the podcast: "The mood and the tone is extremely inviting and a little bit different than anything else I'm listening to. What am I hearing here that I literally couldn't hear anywhere else?" She was not responding to the content. She was responding to the way two people were talking to each other as creators, which is something that rarely happens in professional contexts.

Katie Ann also surfaced something about jealousy and creative life that hit a nerve. She described a feeling that was not quite schadenfreude but related: "Being able to feel neutrally happy without mixing your own self and ego in somebody's success. Maybe that's literally just being happy for someone." The engineering world and the art world both struggle with this. Code reviews and gallery critiques are both spaces where ego and craft collide, and the people who navigate them well are the ones who can separate their identity from their output.

"When you're scaling you have to look at diversity. You can't just think about the next billion as one category of demographic. That's something I find really missing in the language."

The throughline between these conversations is that code and art are not different materials. They are different surfaces of the same material: human expression shaped by tools and constraints. An engineer writing a function and an artist composing a panel are both making decisions about what to include, what to leave out, and how the person encountering their work will move through it. The pretense that these are fundamentally different activities serves nobody. Computational Mama gave herself a new name and found more creative space than her entire design career had offered. Katie Ann moved from outdoor guiding to professional limerick writing to poetry. The lines between disciplines are not just blurry. They were never really there.
